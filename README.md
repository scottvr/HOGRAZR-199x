In 1994 or 1995, a video rental store was closing its doors. What they didn't sell at liquidation was tossed in a bin behind the retail stripmall.  In the bin was a rack of 3.5" shareware games, with Commander Keen, Wolfenstein 3D, even the Doom 1.2 shareware was there, along with a couple of titles nobody had ever heard of. There was one curious disk with  non-descriptive packaging. In the insert visible through the plastic packaging container (the kind you need X-Cato knives and pliers to open, and will almost always injure yourself or the product trying to open) it was labeled as "Hog Raiser", which brings to mind harmless fun in the form of some sort  of farming game.. In fact, the lede below the title said "Frank Farmer. Make the farmer happy."  Which is a pretty odd and not really inviting or exciting, not enticing to make one want to play the game, but certainly causes a bit of curiosity.

Upon cutting through the packaging to get at the paper-labeled diskette itself you notice that here the title is printed differently. The title on the insert and the title on the disk would be pronounced the same, but conjure a completely different image in the mind.  Instead of "Hog Raiser", here it said "hograzor (fatback)" which at first passes by as just a weird misprint due to the similarity to some term you know, perhaps "razorback" but as it echoes in your head, it begins to be a little unsettling. "Hograzor fatback?" What the heck does that mean? And even more so comparing and contrasting the ostensible title of "hograiser" and its tagline "make the farmer" happy. What the hell is in store for the player? That copy was damaged (during opening I am sure) so I never got to experience it myself.

All I know from reports from the few who did play it in its day is that the game would be "finished" in about 3.75 minutes, though notice nobody said it was "easy" or that they had "won" in 3m47s exactly;  only that they were finished. This was possible because the game was a small number of times trials (fleeing, obstacle course, whackamole, skeet, etc type mini games,   I have heard that your character was a small "meat-type hog" (whatever that means) but nobody really liked to talk about it.

>Nobody remembers the title screen correctly.

>That is the first thing you have to understand.

>Everybody agrees there *was* one. Everybody remembers the music beginning immediately, before the screen had fully faded in, but if you ask three people what the title screen looked like you get five answers. One swore it was a cheerful sunrise over a red barn with chunky EGA-style lettering that bounced slightly out of sync with the music. Another insists it was just a black screen with pale pink text and a digitized pig squeal. I remember both of those things being true somehow.

The only thing consistently remembered is the phrase:

> FRANK FARMER NEEDS YOUR HELP

not even punctuated like a real sentence. Just hovering there while the music played.

And that music...

People saying "late 80s club music" undersell how wrong it felt. It had that cheap Roland groovebox energy, sure — synthetic bass stabs, gated snares, a four-on-the-floor kick — but under it was something else. Metallic clanging loops. Rhythmic wet sounds that only later, once you knew more about industrial slaughterhouses, became recognizable. Every thirty seconds or so a calm educational-film narrator would cut in through static:

> "Proper livestock processing ensures maximum yield."

Or:

> "A calm animal produces superior meat."

The samples sounded like they had been copied from VHS tapes ten generations deep. Warbly. Sickly. Occasionally the tape would distort and you'd hear what sounded like screaming beneath the narration.

The graphics looked almost insultingly childish. Big chunky VGA pixels. Bright pink pigs with little white eyes. Cornfields. Smiling suns. The kind of graphics you'd expect from a shovelware edutainment title beside *Math Blaster* in a discount bin.

Then the game started.

You did not control Frank Farmer.

That misunderstanding came from the packaging.

You controlled one of the hogs.

Not a cartoon pig exactly. More like a compressed approximation of one. Thick-bodied. Tiny legs. Pale skin rendered with four shades of pink and gray. The sprite animation had too many frames compared to the rest of the game, which made it look strangely real against the crude backgrounds.

And Frank...

Frank Farmer was never fully shown.

You'd see boots. Gloves. The lower edge of an apron. A flashlight beam. Sometimes a silhouette in a doorway. Once in a while a close-up photograph would flash for a single frame during transitions — an actual digitized human mouth smiling too wide. People argued for years whether those frames were intentional or corruption from bad disks.

The game itself was a sequence of mini-games, but "mini-games" makes them sound fun.

They were more like tests.

The first stage was called FEEDTIME.

You ran around a pen eating as much as possible while synth-pop music chirped unnaturally fast. Troughs appeared randomly. The narrator calmly explained caloric efficiency. Every time you ate, your pig sprite got slightly larger. Not enough to notice immediately. Just enough.

If you failed to eat enough before time expired, a buzzer sounded and a red stamp appeared over your pig:

> LOW QUALITY

Then the screen cut to static.

People assumed that was losing.

It wasn't.

The game continued either way.

The second stage was usually remembered as the worst because it was where players realized what the game actually was.

WEIGH LINE.

You stood on a scale while unseen machinery clanked around you. Hooks moved in the darkness above. Frank's boots paced back and forth at the top edge of the screen. The objective was unclear. Some people tried balancing. Some tried escaping. Nothing helped. Eventually the scale locked and the narrator would say things like:

> "Excellent marbling."

or

> "Substandard composition."

Depending on how well you'd done earlier.

The pig would visibly tremble while idle.

That detail bothered people. The trembling.

Because the rest of the game was so cartoonish, but that animation was subtle and weirdly lifelike.

Then came the running stages.

Long corridors.

Electric prods.

Fence mazes.

You could hear other pigs but never see them. Occasionally another pig sprite would rush past from offscreen, slam into a barrier, and vanish while the music continued uninterrupted.

The soundtrack kept getting better as the game became more upsetting. That's what made it hard to shake off afterward. The basslines were legitimately catchy. Someone described it once as "if Meat Beat Manifesto got hired to score a nightmare version of Oregon Trail."

There was one segment everybody remembered differently but emotionally remembered the same.

The classroom.

The screen would suddenly shift to fake educational-film footage — crude rendered desks, a chalkboard, smiling cartoon livestock seated like children. The narrator discussed "consumer confidence" while your pig sat at a tiny school desk filling in multiple-choice questions with its snout.

Questions like:

> WHAT IS YOUR PURPOSE?

A) PLAY
B) WORK
C) YIELD
D) NONE OF THE ABOVE

No matter what answer you selected, the teacher — never shown directly, only represented by a pointer stick entering frame — marked it incorrect until you chose YIELD.

At some point the music would synchronize with machinery sounds so perfectly that you stopped distinguishing them.

The ending is where stories diverge hardest.

Because the game adapted slightly.

Not intelligently — this was DOS shareware in 1994, not some haunted AI cartridge — but enough branching existed that different players saw different final sequences.

But all endings converged eventually.

The final stage was called FATBACK.

Just that word centered onscreen.

No instructions.

The music slowed down dramatically. Same song, half speed now. Distorted.

You moved through a dark corridor toward a rectangle of white light while statistical information scrolled overhead:

LIVE WEIGHT
USABLE YIELD
LOSS PERCENTAGE
COMPLIANCE SCORE

Frank Farmer finally spoke here.

Not through text. Through horrible crunchy Sound Blaster digitized audio.

People expected some deranged screaming farmer.

But he sounded exhausted.

Almost sympathetic.

Like somebody at the end of a very long shift.

He would say things like:

> "C'mon now."

Or:

> "Don't make this difficult."

Or the line everybody remembered:

> "Make the farmer happy."

And then...

Nothing graphic happened.

That was the genius of it.

The screen became overwhelmingly white. The music clipped into static. There was a single loud mechanical impact sound.

Then silence.

A DOS prompt.

No GAME OVER.
No TRY AGAIN.
No high scores.

Just:

```text
C:\HOGRAZR>
```

The game had exited itself.

That was why people said they were "finished" in three and a half minutes instead of saying they won.

Because it did not feel like losing a game.

It felt like participating in a process.

The reason I replayed it was simple: I didn't believe what I'd experienced the first time. I thought maybe I had projected meaning onto bad shareware assembled by some bored industrial-music weirdo with Turbo Pascal and too much Nine Inch Nails in his bloodstream.

But the second playthrough was worse.

Because once you knew where it was going, you noticed the details.

The cheerful color palette slowly desaturating.
The way the pig sprite gradually stopped blinking.
The hidden messages occasionally flickering in the status bar:

> YIELD IS TRUTH

or

> THANK YOU FOR YOUR CONTRIBUTION

The fact that the soundtrack was exactly long enough to cover one complete playthrough with no looping.

That last detail haunted me more than anything else.

Whoever made it knew precisely how long the experience was supposed to last. Like a ride. Or a ritual.

And here's the part that bothered me for years afterward:

The game was shareware.

Meaning somebody expected people to *register* it.
